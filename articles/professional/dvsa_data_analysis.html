A glimpse at the UK driving test data

I recently booked a driving test to get my UK license. These tests are conducted all year round by the Driver and Vehicle Standards Agency (DVSA) at its test centres all over the country. My current driving license is from India and my experience with that test was really good. It was more or less a cakewalk for me and therefore, I was pretty confident of clearing the UK driving test in my first attempt. However, amidst a conversation, several of my friends mentioned the level of difficulty of the UK’s driving tests and how only a small margin of drivers clear it in their first attempt. To be honest, this piece of information took a slight hit at my confidence. However, before going down the alley filled with self-doubts and loss of morale, I decided to take an objective look at the driving tests in the UK. Fortunately, the DVSA website provides sufficient data to facilitate my curiosity. These data sets contain year-wise data for the total tests conducted and total passes per gender for each of the centres. Along with the same, I also gathered some additional datasets specifically for first-time test takers, pass rates for automatic vehicles and another indicating their ethnicity. Ethnicity data consisted of pass percentage aggregate for each year while first-time test-taker data, in addition, contained the number of zero faults made by both genders.

A closer look at these data would give me an opportunity to validate the narrative I heard from my friends. Therefore, I decided to perform a preliminary analysis using my favourite data visualiser - Microsoft Power BI. One of the most interesting elements in Power BI is its ability to create interactive dashboards. Further, relations can be easily created between multiple data sources to generate quick and meaningful plots that allow dynamic interactions. But, before I delve into data cleaning and analysis, it is critical to formulate the objectives and questions that are to be answered. I believe creating an Aims grid would provide a concise and focused route map for the whole project.

Purposes/ Benefits Clients/ Stakeholders
  • Average pass rate.
  • Analyse the correlation between the overall pass rate and the gender of the driver.
  • Pass percentage based on the gender on the first attempt.
  • Find passing rates for different ethnicities in the country.
  • Analyse if specific months have an impact on the result of the driving test.
  • Primary stakeholders are pupils, instructors, examiners and DVSA.
  • Secondary stakeholders could be friends, family, employers, other road users, government etc.
End Result Success Criteria
An interactive Dashboard that would provide data-driven insights about the relation between gender and pass rate.
  • Dashboard providing insight into whether any bias based on gender exists.
  • The stakeholders will be able to know if selecting a particular test centre further increases the chance of passing the driving test.

Essentially, I am trying to find if there are other factors besides driving skills that influence the results of a driving test. However, the vast data given by DVSA are not in a format suitable for creating plots. So the foremost thing was to parse these files and tabulate the data into a more ‘plottable’ format. I used a python script to do the same. It was somewhat a tedious task due to the non-uniform formatting. If the reader is interested in getting my pre-processed data, they can get it from here. With this data, I created a dashboard to understand it better, make relations and consolidate values based on features to bring out conclusive ideas from them. The data required for analyses has been allocated into four different tables. The four different tables relate to (1) overall pass percentage (2) automatic vehicle pass percentage (3) pass based on gender and ethnicity (4) the number of faults in the first time pass again based on gender from 2007-2021 for each of the test centres.

Dashboard 1 is a visual representation of the overall pass percentage dataset for each of the centres from the year 2007-2021. The dashboard consists of two slicers and five plots and can be downloaded here. The very first slicer is for selecting a particular year, followed by the slicer for each centre. The percentages shown in each of the plots are the median of the values for all months in a year. Median was chosen to avoid any outliers and to have a robust estimate of the expected pass percentage.

I started by having a look at the median pass percentage of each year for all centres combined. This plot will give a clear indication of the nationwide pass percentage and also a peek if the agency has relaxed its standard over the years. Further, I plotted the pass percentage of both genders separately to see if sex influences the metric. From the plot, we can see that the overall pass percentage stayed nearly the same throughout all years and there is almost a constant difference between both genders. The almost constant pass percentage over the years indicate that the DVSA standards have been the same for the period considered. Though I checked the trend with automatic vehicle pass percentages, I couldn’t find anything different.

Now since I understood that there exists a difference between the pass percentage of men and women, I wanted to see if the same trend exists for first-timers as well. I kind of wished for an opposite trend for this chart. But to my disappointment, I witnessed the same trend for first-timers as well. Throughout the years, the data shows that males outperform females. Additionally, more male drivers have managed to pass the driving test in their first attempts with zero faults than female drivers. Personally, I feel, one of the reasons for men to outperform women in driving tests might be the natural affinity of the gender towards mechanical devices from a young age. Though there still exists the prenotion of men being better drivers, the number of female drivers taking the test has increased in the past couple of decades. This is evidently seen from the plot given in Figure 2. The drop in numbers from the year 2020 is an obvious aftereffect of the pandemic.

Again, being an immigrant in the UK, I got too curious to see whether ethnicity affects the pass percentage. To my surprise and relief, there was no indication in the data of any such differences. If we take a closer look at the test data based on ethnicity, the majority of the test takers belong to the ‘no preference’ or “prefer not to say” category. Apart from them, the rest of the data looks like an equal distribution amongst various ethnicities: i.e., no specific ethnicity is at a disadvantage.

I don’t know why, but the results so far motivated me to see if picking a particular centre would be beneficial. Dashboard 2 investigates the answer to this question. Since there are numerous test centres, I thought of plotting the data from the top and bottom five centres when sorted based on pass percentage. I intended to see if there is a significant difference between the top and bottom five. If there isn't, then there is no point in checking for the other centres. I used the ‘Top N’ function in Power BI to filter the data for the top and bottom five test centres. I was expecting a trend similar to the national median. But to my surprise, the results for the top five centres were 12 percent higher than the national average of 49.8% (shown in chart 1 of dashboard 2). However, the bottom 5 had their median pass percentage (50.45%) almost equal to the national median. These inferences led me to include the population density for each region into the existing data for the 10 centres. This would help deduce if there is any correlation between the population density and the pass percentages. My thought was if there are fewer people, then correspondingly traffic and the number of junctions will also be less. This reduction would mean that driving in these places may be relatively easy.

The top five centres include Llandrindod (20 persons per sq km), Malton (2937per sq km), Whitby (875.4 per sq km), Dorchester (133 per sq km), Brecon (25 per sq km). The bottom five test centres included Gairloch (220.9 per sq km), Dorchester county town (13 per sq km), Oxford (Kassam Stadium, 3270 per sq km), Halifax Maltings (1077 per sq km), Inverness (Longman drive, 2958.2 per sq km). It can be seen that the top and bottom five, both contain high and low populated regions. Hence, it cannot be conclusively said whether there is a correlation between pass percentage and population density in the region of the test centres. Further, when we plot the graph for pass percentages in the first attempt throughout years it shows a somewhat straight line with an overlap of both the genders. So I am still curious about the possible difference from the national median. This led me to check if the test-taking month had any influence on the pass percentage. However, I was not able to find any such trend based on the months. Though there was a drop in percentage in February for all top five centres, the rest of the year looked more or less the same. Similarly, for the bottom five, test-taking month never influenced the outcome.

In short, the dashboards were created to fulfil each of the objectives stated in the AIMS Grid as well as provide a broad perspective on the driving test results based on various factors. The other benefit of the interactive characteristic is that the stakeholders can retrieve data and visualisation based on specific years and centres from the dashboard. The object introspection shows that the stories I heard were true and are now backed with evidence. The whole process has made me more mindful of the challenges in the UK driving test. Irrespective of the results obtained, I strongly believe extra efforts and proper guidance from a great instructor will help one go that extra mile and clear the test.
